## Post 1: Business Outcomes vs. Functional Outcomes Trap

June 2026

Link: 

[https://www.linkedin.com/posts/anthonypierri_im-not-saying-all-marketers-are-liars-activity-7470117293679763458-T4kt?utm_source=share&utm_medium=member_desktop&rcm=ACoAAE8m65QBcqY4_RmKPosShZbFiMFHEs1jO2s](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/anthonypierri_im-not-saying-all-marketers-are-liars-activity-7470117293679763458-T4kt?utm_source=share&utm_medium=member_desktop&rcm=ACoAAE8m65QBcqY4_RmKPosShZbFiMFHEs1jO2s)

I'm not saying all marketers are liars, 

but... 😬 😬 😬 

A LOT of what we do is promise things that go far beyond what we can guarantee. 

We've been trained to "sell the outcome." 

And in business contexts that means selling the BUSINESS outcome.

Things like "getting more customers," "increasing revenue," "business growth," etc.

Here's the problem: 

business outcomes are never driven by one thing

Every BUSINESS outcome is determined by an interlocking set of factors that span the entire BUSINESS. 

Whether or not a B2B software company "wins more customers" is a function of...

 • Precise ICP targeting and segmentation

 • Marketing reach within target roles and industries

 • Executive thought leadership and content

 • Case studies, logos, and peer validation

 • Brand credibility

 • Differentiation from legacy tools or point solutions

 • Positioning [WHAT WE DO]

 • Competitive landscape

 • Free trials, pilot programs, or POCs

 • Pricing and contract flexibility

 • Sales execution and account-based strategy

 • Tech stack & ecosystem fit

 • Regulatory and compliance readiness (SOC 2, HIPAA)

 • Procurement process alignment 

 • Product breadth and roadmap confidence

 • Channel or partner integrations

 • Market timing and budget cycles

 • Platform stability & uptime

 • User experience

 • Implementation onboarding support

And on and on and on. 

No product/service impacts all these factors. 

And so... no product can guarantee a business-level outcome. 

To tell someone that adopting a CRM will make them "win more customers" is simply a lie. 

They will win more customers provided that... 

ALL THOSE OTHER FACTORS THAT ARE UNRELATED TO A CRM ARE WORKING PROPERLY.

If you take someone who is just starting out and has no product yet and they buy a CRM...

...they will not "win more customers." 

So what are TRUE outcomes that CRMs can guarantee? 

They can help teams be more organized. 

They can help teams do forecasting. 

They can help teams see all customer touchpoints.

These are the REAL reasons customers purchase CRMs. 

Notice that these outcomes are very "functional." 

This is because all B2B software is, in essence, "workflow software." 

Workers get software to help them do their work

(hence it's "workflow software") 

A rule of thumb... 

if you would feel very uncomfortable putting "increase revenue" in your contract as a guaranteed outcome...

...maybe avoid saying it in your marketing materials. 

I understand you might think I am a hack for saying this. 

But my co-founder and I do roughly $1.7m a year as a three-person consultancy, and we have never once promised any of these business outcomes. 

We ONLY promise what we can GUARANTEE: 

→ a clarified positioning strategy documented in a crystal clear homepage

If everything else is working,

then you WILL get those good business outcomes. 

But it won't be as a result of us:

it will be the result of us + the thousands of other things you're doing that we won't touch.

### Annotation

- Demonstrates outcome-based messaging and positioning principles in B2B software.
- Distinguishes between functional outcomes that software can directly influence and broader business outcomes that depend on multiple variables.
- Reinforces the importance of credibility and specificity when communicating product value.

## Post 2: The Three Strategic Positioning Playbooks

June 2026

Link: 

[https://www.linkedin.com/posts/anthonypierri_people-overcomplicate-positioning-there-activity-7468383258863423488-Phf2?utm_source=share&utm_medium=member_desktop&rcm=ACoAAE8m65QBcqY4_RmKPosShZbFiMFHEs1jO2s](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/anthonypierri_people-overcomplicate-positioning-there-activity-7468383258863423488-Phf2?utm_source=share&utm_medium=member_desktop&rcm=ACoAAE8m65QBcqY4_RmKPosShZbFiMFHEs1jO2s)

People overcomplicate positioning. 

There are really just three strategies...

Strategy 1: Position in a mature category against existing vendors

You place yourself in an established category and argue why you're better than the competition. 

The upside:

Customers already have budget for it, actively search for it, and don't need educating. 

The risk: 

Mature categories have big, entrenched players. Without serious differentiation and resources, you'll struggle to make the shortlist.

Choose this if... 

• the category is large enough to meet your growth goals

• you have the resources to compete head-to-head for the whole market

• OR you're willing to focus an underserved subsegment 

Strategy 2: Take an existing (but small) category to a new audience

You position in a known (but likely nascent) category and sell it to people who haven't heard of it yet.

(E.g. Skrillex bringing Dubstep to the mainstream. DocuSign didn't invent e-signature, but they focused on selling it to companies that used paper. Dropbox didn't invent file sharing, but they popularized it.) 

The upside: 

• Less direct competition, more greenfield opportunity

The risk: 

• You have to educate the market rather than waiting for inbound demand

• You also inherit the category's reputation (for good or bad)

Choose this if... 

• you believe the category will grow and you can become a leader as it solidifies

• you have the marketing patience to play the long game

Strategy 3: Create your own category

You refuse to fit into anyone else's box and seek to build a new one. 

The upside: 

You get full control to define both the problem and solution framing. 

The risks: 

• you need to pick the right-sized problem (too broad and it's unbelievable) 

• first movers rarely win (Airbnb beat Vrbo, Google beat AltaVista), and large incumbents may simply copy your blueprint once you've proven the market, as Microsoft did to Slack.

Choose this if... 

• you genuinely believe any existing category label would hurt more than help

• you're prepared to invest years building awareness from scratch

### Annotation

- Demonstrates three distinct positioning strategies for B2B products and SaaS companies.
- Explains the trade-offs between competing in established categories, expanding emerging categories, and creating new categories.
- Reinforces positioning as a strategic business decision rather than a messaging exercise.
